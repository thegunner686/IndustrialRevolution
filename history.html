Timeline

The Steam Engine is Invented
Thomas Newcomen invented the first prototype steam engine. Unfortunately, it wasn’t very useful at its inception as it was quite inefficient and lackluster. That being said, steam engines would soon go on to transform the very fabric of the Industrial Revolution. By mechanizing processes that used to be done by hand.
1712
The Spinning Jenny is Invented
James Hargreaves, a British weaver and carpenter invented the spinning jenny in 1764. This machine could spin more than one ball of yarn or thread at a time, that in turn greatly increased the amount of cloth in the textile industry (more efficient system than simply hand weaving. One man’s work now equated over ten-fold of hand spinning). Sadly, led to use of child labor to fix the broke yarn at times.
1764
James Watt Improves the Steam Engine
A man from Scotland, James Watt, on who a unit is named after created a much more efficient design for a steam engine. This was one of the, if not the most important invention of the Industrial Revolution. Steam managed to power the first trains, factories, and steamboats. Now, the steam engine was in full swing as James Watt’s version could actually be put to use in a cost-efficient sense. Factories soon sprung up to take advantage of this new technology.
1769
Eli Whitney Patents the Cotton Gin
In 1794, Eli Whitney created a machine that makes it a lot easier to separate the pesky cotton seeds from the wanted cotton fiber. In fact, it greatly reduced the amount of time it took to clean cotton. It further helped the southern states make a lot more money from cotton crops. Unfortunately, this lead to an increase in the demand for cheap labor. Hence why an influx of slaves came to the New World (and why the south’s economy was cotton dependent).
1794
Samuel Morse Invents the Telegraph
In 1844, Samuel Morse (recognize morse code?) invented the telegraph. It allowed messages to be sent over long distances and quickly over a wire. Interestingly enough, by 1860, the telegraph wires stretched from the far east coast of the United States to west of the Mississippi River. In fact, this is one of the advantages the Union had over the Confederate States as they could communicate efficiently over long distances.
1844
Elias Howe Invents the Sewing Machine
During the time period when people were forced to make their own clothes at home or rely on paying someone else to sew them by hand, this invention was a welcome one indeed. In 1846, Elias Howe invented the sewing machine. This allowed clothes to be made in large factories and much more cheaply as well. A very exciting prospect for budget conscious shoppers.
1846
Elisha Otis invents the elevator safety break.
Elevators were already invented by 1853, but people worried about elevator cars falling. Elisha Otis invents a safety break to prevent them from falling if a cable breaks, making people feel more confident about using elevators in tall buildings.
1853
The Bessemer Method for processing steel is invented.
Henry Bessemer invents a process for making steel out of iron. Having a way to make steel more quickly and more cheaply helps the production of building and leads to the growth of cities.
January 1855
Alfred Nobel creates dynamite.
Alfred Nobel invents dynamite, which is a safer way to blast holes in mountains or the ground than simply lighting black powder. Dynamite is important in clearing paths to build things such as roads and railroad tracks.
1866
Louis Pasteur develops vaccines for diseases.
A chemist named Louis Pasteur believed that germs caused disease. Using this information, he created vaccines that helped prevent many common diseases, which helped people live longer.
January 1870
Alexander Graham Bell patents the telephone.
He may not have invented the telephone, but Alexander Graham Bell was the first to get a patent for it. Being able to speak to people over a telephone wire greatly changes the way the world communicates.
March 1876
Thomas Edison uses a light bulb to light a lamp.
Not the first man to create a light bulb, Thomas Edison created a light bulb that lasted longer than other designs and showed it off by lighting a lamp. Edison's light bulbs allow people to do many things at night, such as work, that used to only happen during the day.
October 1879
The Brooklyn Bridge opens.
After 13 years of construction, the Brooklyn Bridge is finished in New York City. At the time, it was the longest suspension bridge in the world.
May 1883
Orville Wright makes the first powered airplane flight.
Using an engine that they invented, Orville and Wilbur Wright invent the first plane that is not powered by wind. Orville flies the plane for 12 seconds over a beach in North Carolina.
December 1903
Henry Ford creates the Model T.
Henry Ford creates a type of car called the Model T. It is much cheaper than other cars because it is made on an assembly line, allowing many more people to buy cars.
